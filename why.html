// THE PROBLEM, DOCUMENTED

Why most backtests mislead — including honest ones

🎲

Try enough, win by luck

Test enough variations of a strategy and one will look great by chance alone. Researchers have shown impressive simulated performance is easily achieved this way — and tends to evaporate out-of-sample.1

📈

No fair baseline

"Price bounced after my level" proves little in a rising market — everything bounced. Without comparing against a random comparable trade, market drift masquerades as skill.2

🔮

Leaking the future

Look-ahead bias — accidentally letting tomorrow's information into today's decision — is one of the most common and subtle backtest errors, and it manufactures edges that never existed.3

🔢

One seductive number

A single Sharpe ratio or win rate hides how many trials produced it. Properly adjusted for multiple testing, reported performance deserves a haircut — sometimes a severe one.4

None of this requires bad faith. These are failure modes of the process, which is why the safeguards below were invented.

// THE LANDSCAPE

Today you choose: accessible, or rigorous

📱

Accessible tools

Charting platforms and no-code backtesters are excellent at what they aim for: fast, visual strategy testing anyone can use. But the statistical validation — out-of-sample discipline, baselines, significance — is left to the user, and built-in optimizers make it effortless to run the many trials that inflate results.

💻

Code frameworks

Python and institutional-grade platforms can absolutely do this right — the best even warn about overfitting. But the matched baselines and significance testing are yours to design, implement, and interpret. It's a toolkit for quants and engineers, not a product for traders.

🎓

Academic methods

The statistics for asking "is this result real, or the luckiest of many tried?" exist and are well accepted — reality-check baselines, superior predictive ability tests, multiple-comparisons control.5 They live in journals and quant shops, not in anything a trader can pick up.

The gap: nothing accessible applies the accepted safeguards by default. That's what we're building.

// OUR APPROACH

The gold standard, on by default

⚖

A matched baseline

Every setup is judged against a random comparable trade — the "reality check" posture pioneered in the data-snooping literature — so drift can't pose as edge.5

🔒

Locked before the reveal

Rules are committed in writing before out-of-sample data is touched, and the verdict lands on a holdout period never used to build the idea — the discipline the backtesting literature prescribes.4

📊

Multiple-testing control

When many variations are tried, the math accounts for it — established false-discovery and stepwise-testing methods, not a leaderboard of lucky winners.6

💬

Plain-English verdicts

Edge, no edge, or not enough evidence — in reward:risk and win rate, with honest uncertainty ranges instead of one seductive number.

We didn't invent the math — and you should distrust anyone who implies they did. These methods are decades-old, peer-reviewed, and standard practice in serious quantitative research. What we're building is the packaging: that rigor applied automatically to setups you describe in plain terms, so you benefit from the process without having to be the statistician.
