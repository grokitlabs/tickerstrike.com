// THE PROBLEM, DOCUMENTED

Why most backtests mislead — including honest ones

🎲

Try enough, win by luck

Test enough variations of a strategy and one will look great by chance alone. Researchers have shown impressive simulated performance is easily achieved this way — and tends to evaporate out-of-sample.1

📈

No fair baseline

"Price bounced after my level" proves little in a rising market — everything bounced. Without comparing against a comparable trade taken at a random time under similar conditions, market drift masquerades as skill.

🔮

Leaking the future

Look-ahead bias — accidentally letting tomorrow's information into today's decision — is one of the most common and subtle backtest errors, and it manufactures edges that never existed.2

🔢

One seductive number

A single Sharpe ratio or win rate hides how many trials produced it — and whether costs were included. Properly adjusted for multiple testing, reported performance deserves a haircut, sometimes a severe one.3

None of this requires bad faith. These are failure modes of the process — which is why the safeguards below were developed.

// THE LANDSCAPE

Today you choose: accessible, or rigorous

📱

Accessible tools

Charting platforms and no-code backtesters are excellent at what they aim for: fast, visual strategy testing anyone can use. But the statistical validation — out-of-sample discipline, baselines, significance — is left to the user, and built-in optimizers make it effortless to run the many trials that inflate results.

💻

Code frameworks

Python and institutional-grade platforms can absolutely do this right — the best even warn about overfitting. But the baselines and significance testing are yours to design, implement, and interpret correctly. It's a toolkit for quants and engineers, not a product for traders.

🎓

Academic methods

The statistics for asking "is this result real, or the luckiest of many tried?" are well accepted — data-snooping tests, false-discovery control, out-of-sample protocols.4 They live in journals and quant shops, not in anything a trader can pick up.

The gap: nothing accessible applies the accepted safeguards by default. That's what we're building.

// OUR APPROACH

Well-accepted safeguards, on by default

⚖

A matched baseline

Every setup is judged against placebo trades — same instrument, same period, same trade mechanics, random timing — so a drifting market can't pose as skill. How the baseline is matched is documented, because a sloppy baseline can manufacture edge too.

🔒

Locked before the reveal

Hypothesis, data window, and success criterion are committed before the test runs; confirmation happens on holdout data never used to build the idea. In the tradition of the data-snooping literature4 — pre-registration as process, not paperwork.

📊

Multiple-testing control

Test twenty variations and one "wins" by luck. We correct for it with established false-discovery-rate methods5, and report confidence intervals from the data — not a single flattering point estimate.

💬

Three honest verdicts

Edge, no edge, or not enough evidence — in plain reward:risk and win rate. Underpowered tests say so instead of pretending. And no result counts as tradeable until costs are part of the picture.

We didn't invent the math — and you should distrust anyone who implies they did. These methods are decades-old, peer-reviewed, and standard practice in serious quantitative research. Our contribution is the packaging: that rigor applied by default to setups you describe in trader terms, so you benefit from the process without having to be the statistician.

What exists today, and what we're building

✅

Working now

The research engine — placebo baselines, pre-registered studies, holdout confirmation, FDR correction, bootstrap confidence intervals — runs today on our own studies. It has found a real, confirmed edge and, just as importantly, killed several appealing ideas that turned out to be nothing.

🔧

Being built

The product around it: describing a setup in trader terms instead of code, running the safeguards without an operator, and verdicts anyone can read. That's the bridge from a rigorous internal engine to a tool you can use.

🧭

Open problems

We're honest about the hard parts too — like keeping holdout data trustworthy when many people test ideas against the same history. Problems like these shape the design; pretending they don't exist is how backtesting tools end up lying.
